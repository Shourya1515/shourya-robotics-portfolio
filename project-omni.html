Omni-Directional Robot with Kinematics & Obstacle Avoidance

ROS / ROS2 · Gazebo · Omni-Directional Kinematics · LiDAR-Based Obstacle Avoidance

A bio-inspired omni-directional robot designed to move smoothly in any direction, combining full kinematic modeling with a ROS-based control stack and LiDAR-driven obstacle avoidance in simulation.

Gallery

Project Overview

This project explores the design of a bio-inspired, omni-directional mobile robot. The platform is capable of translating and rotating in any direction, making it well suited for tight indoor environments where conventional differential-drive robots struggle to maneuver.

The robot’s mechanical concept is modeled in CAD and then represented in URDF for use with ROS and Gazebo. The focus of the project is on understanding omni-directional kinematics, mapping body-frame velocities to individual wheel speeds, and validating those relationships through simulation.

Kinematics & Control

  • Developed the full kinematic model and Jacobian for an omni-directional base, relating desired linear and angular body velocities to individual wheel angular speeds.
  • Implemented inverse kinematics to convert high-level motion commands (vx, vy, ω) into wheel-level control inputs.
  • Verified motion primitives (pure translation, pure rotation, diagonal motion) inside simulation to confirm the correctness of the kinematic mapping.

ROS & Simulation Stack

  • Modeled the robot in URDF with links, joints, and collision geometry, and brought it into Gazebo for physics-based simulation.
  • Used ROS to integrate wheel controllers, TF frames, LiDAR, and RViz visualization, enabling introspection of robot pose, odometry, and sensor data in real time.
  • Implemented a ROS node that subscribes to /cmd_vel and applies the omni-directional kinematic equations to generate per-wheel velocity commands.

Obstacle Avoidance

Instead of following objects, the robot performs reactive obstacle avoidance using LiDAR data. A dedicated ROS node subscribes to the /scan topic, partitions the environment into angular sectors, and monitors the minimum distance to obstacles in front, left, and right of the robot.

When an obstacle is detected within a safety threshold, the node reduces forward velocity and commands a lateral or rotational motion using the omni-directional kinematics. This allows the robot to sidestep or rotate away from obstacles while preserving overall goal-directed movement, resulting in smooth, collision-free navigation in cluttered scenes.

Outcome

The omni-directional robot successfully demonstrates holonomic motion and LiDAR-based obstacle avoidance in ROS and Gazebo. The project highlights end-to-end skills in kinematic modeling, simulation, and ROS-based perception and control, and serves as a foundation for future work on higher-level planning and multi-robot coordination.
